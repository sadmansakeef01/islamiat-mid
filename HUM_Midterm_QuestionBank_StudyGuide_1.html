Conquest of Makkah (8 AH)
Casus belli, Abu Sufyan's surrender, Bilal's Adhan, the amnesty (Quran 17:81).
▼
Casus Belli (cause of war)
  • Quraysh broke the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah
  • They helped the Banu Bakr tribe attack the allied Khuza'ah tribe
  • This violation gave the Prophet (PBUH) just cause to march on Makkah
The March on Makkah
  • On 1 Jan 630 CE, the Prophet led 10,000 Muslims — the largest force Madinah had ever mobilised
  • The city fell almost entirely without combat
Abu Sufyan's Surrender
  • Abu Sufyan was the Prophet's lifelong enemy and a Quraysh leader
  • At the city gates, the Prophet forgave him completely
  • Abu Sufyan embraced Islam on the spot — mercy winning over even the fiercest opponent
Bilal's Adhan — significance
  • 360 idols were removed from the Kaabah, restoring pure monotheism
  • Bilal (RA), a freed black slave, gave the Adhan from atop the Kaabah
  • This symbolised racial equality (a former slave honoured above Makkah's elite) and Islam's total victory over idolatry
Declaration of Amnesty
  • The Prophet declared: "Go — you are all free!"
  • Linked to Quran 17:81 — truth has come, falsehood has vanished
  • Result: the conquest is remembered as a mercy-driven, near-bloodless victory

5
Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH)
Four stages, three unfavourable terms, the real strategic gains (Quran 48:1).
▼
Four Stages of the Event
  • The Mission — 1,400 Muslims set out for Umrah; Quraysh mobilised forces to block them at the Hudaybiyyah plain
  • Bay'at ar-Ridwan (Pledge of the Tree) — a rumour that Uthman (RA) had been killed sparked believers to pledge to fight to the end
  • Terms Agreed — a ten-year truce was signed; Muslims would return that year; Quraysh implicitly recognised the Islamic state as real
  • Divine Verdict — Quran 48:1 declared: "Indeed, We have granted you a clear victory" — the treaty itself was the triumph
Three Unfavourable Terms
  • Muslims had to return that year without completing Umrah
  • They could only come back the following year, and only stay 3 days
  • Muslim apostates who fled to Quraysh were returned, but the reverse did not apply
Real Strategic Gains
  • Islam spread more in these 2 peaceful years than in the previous 18 years of conflict
  • The Muslim population doubled before the conquest of Makkah
  • Quraysh later broke the treaty (helping Banu Bakr attack Khuza'ah), giving just cause to conquer Makkah
  • An apparently "losing" treaty became the doorway to total victory

9
Muakhaat (Brotherhood)
What it was, its five forms of solidarity, why it was a prerequisite for everything else.
▼
What Muakhaat Was
  • The Prophet (PBUH) paired each Ansari (local Madinan) with a Muhajir (Makkan migrant)
  • Each pair shared wealth, home, and livelihood — like real brothers, with no blood relation
Five Types of Solidarity
  • Paired Fellowship — sharing wealth and livelihood generously between partners
  • Faith Over Blood — bonds built on shared faith, not tribe or ethnicity — revolutionary for tribal Arabia
  • Willing Generosity — some Ansar gave portions of their own farmland to their new brothers
  • Solidarity in All States — partners stood together in both sorrow and happiness
  • Pluralistic Extension — later widened through the Constitution of Madinah to include Jews and others as one city-state
Why It Was a Prerequisite
  • The Constitution of Madinah needed real trust between former strangers — Muakhaat built that trust first
  • The new Islamic economic system (Zakat, treasury, mutual support) needed people already willing to share wealth — which Muakhaat had normalised
  • Without this base of brotherhood, neither political unity nor economic redistribution could have functioned

Growth of the Muslim Army
Three campaigns (force size, lesson, outcome) + the moral discipline code.
▼
Badr (2 AH, 624 CE)
  • Only 313 Muslims fought, outnumbered roughly 3:1
  • They won decisively
  • Lesson: victory comes from faith and conviction, not numbers
Uhud (3 AH, 625 CE)
  • 700 Muslims fought
  • 70 were martyred after the archers left their post, disobeying direct orders
  • Lesson: discipline and obedience matter more than early advantage
Tabuk (9 AH, 631 CE)
  • 30,000 soldiers — the largest force the Prophet ever personally mobilised
  • Shows how dramatically Muslim strength had grown by the end of his life
  • Outcome: a show of strength on the Byzantine frontier, no major battle needed
Moral Discipline Code
  • Strict moral standards required of every soldier
  • Any breach of discipline led to full personal accountability
  • Absolutely forbidden to harm: women, children, monks, the elderly, and crops — even in active war

Ulum al-Quran — Five Sub-Disciplines
Wahy, Tafsir, Qira'at, Nasikh wal-Mansukh, I'jaz al-Quran + one scholar each.
▼
  • Ulum al-Quran = the master discipline; without it no other Islamic science can be properly studied
1. Wahy (Revelation)
  • Studies how, when, and to whom verses were revealed, including Asbab al-Nuzul (occasions of revelation)
  • Basis: Quran 17:106
2. Tafsir (Exegesis)
  • Systematic explanation of Quran's meaning using Hadith, Arabic, and juristic principles
  • Foundational text: Ibn Kathir's Tafsir al-Quran al-Azim
3. Qira'at (Recitations)
  • The 7 (or 10) authenticated modes of recitation, each traced by unbroken chain to the Prophet
  • Foundational text: Al-Shatibi's Hirz al-Amani
4. Nasikh wal-Mansukh (Abrogation)
  • Identifies which rulings supersede earlier ones — vital for correct legal application
  • Associated scholar: Ibn Hazm
5. I'jaz al-Quran (Miraculous Nature)
  • Proves the Quran's literary/scientific miracle that no human can replicate — basis: Quran 2:23
  • Foundational scholar: Al-Baqillani

Tasawwuf (Islamic Spirituality)
Definition, Quranic basis, Ihsan from the Hadith of Jibril, key scholar, why Fiqh alone isn't enough.
▼
What Tasawwuf Is
  • Also called Sufism or Ihsan
  • Focuses on purification of the soul (tazkiyat al-nafs)
  • Aims to make every act of worship sincere, not just outwardly correct
Quranic Basis & Definition of Ihsan
  • Rooted in Quran 2:238
  • Defined in the Hadith of Jibril (Sahih Muslim): "to worship Allah as if you see Him"
  • Considered the highest of the three levels of religion: Islam, Iman, Ihsan
Most Prominent Scholar
  • Imam Al-Ghazali
  • Foundational work: Ihya Ulum al-Din ("Revival of the Religious Sciences")
  • This book combines Fiqh (law) with spirituality, showing the two need each other
Why Fiqh Without Tasawwuf Is Empty
  • A person can pray, fast, and follow every legal rule correctly
  • But still carry arrogance, envy, or showing-off (riya) in the heart
  • These inner diseases invalidate the spiritual value of outwardly correct acts
  • Tasawwuf disciplines the heart so correct actions are also sincere ones
Quick Recall
Tazkiyat al-nafs = soul purificationIhsan = "worship as if you see Him"Al-Ghazali — Ihya Ulum al-DinFiqh w/o Tasawwuf = empty acts

Fiqh — Usul al-Fiqh & the Four Sunni Schools
Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas + Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali compared.
▼
Four Sources of Fiqh (Usul al-Fiqh)
  • Quran — direct divine legislation
  • Sunnah — the Prophet's practice and sayings, explaining Quranic rulings
  • Ijma — scholarly consensus on unsettled issues
  • Qiyas — analogical reasoning, applying a known ruling to a new but similar case
Hanafi School
  • Founder: Imam Abu Hanifa (699–767 CE) · Region: Iraq, Turkey, South Asia, Egypt
  • Principle: emphasises ra'y (reasoned opinion) and qiyas; flexible in commercial law
Maliki School
  • Founder: Imam Malik ibn Anas (711–795 CE) · Region: North/West Africa, Spain
  • Principle: prioritises the living practice of Madinah's people and Maslaha (public welfare)
Shafi'i School
  • Founder: Imam al-Shafi'i (767–820 CE) · Region: Southeast Asia, Egypt, East Africa
  • Principle: first to systematically codify Usul al-Fiqh; strictly prioritises Quran & Sunnah
Hanbali School
  • Founder: Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (780–855 CE) · Region: Saudi Arabia, Gulf States
  • Principle: most conservative; restricts qiyas, strongly resists religious innovation (bid'ah)
Quick Recall
Quran→Sunnah→Ijma→QiyasHanafi = reasoned opinionMaliki = Madinah practiceShafi'i = first to codify UsulHanbali = strictest

Six Pillars of Iman (Aqeedah)
All six pillars with one Quranic reference each, plus Aqeedah vs Fiqh.
▼
The Six Pillars of Iman (Arkan al-Iman)
  • Belief in Allah — absolute Tawhid: One, unique, without partners (Quran 112:1–4)
  • Belief in Angels — beings of light who never disobey Allah (Quran 2:285)
  • Belief in Divine Books — Torah, Psalms, Gospel, Quran; only the Quran is unaltered (Quran 4:136)
  • Belief in Prophets — 124,000 sent to all nations; Muhammad (PBUH) is the last (Quran 33:40)
  • Belief in the Last Day — resurrection, judgment, Sirat, Jannah, Jahannam are certain (Quran 99:1–8)
  • Belief in Divine Decree — all events occur by Allah's will, yet free will still exists (Quran 54:49)
Aqeedah vs Fiqh
  • Aqeedah = what a Muslim must believe with certainty (yaqin) — the unseen realities of faith
  • Fiqh = what a Muslim must do — practical legal rulings governing actions
  • A scholar who masters Fiqh but neglects Aqeedah produces rulings that are procedurally correct but spiritually hollow — law without the foundation of belief behind it
Quick Recall
Allah · Angels · Books · Prophets · Last Day · Decree124,000 prophetsAqeedah = belief, Fiqh = action

Islamic Art & Architecture — Aesthetic of Tawhid
Four features, three monuments from three continents, no figurative art rule.
▼
Four Distinctive Features & Their Meaning
  • Geometric patterns — tessellations express Allah's infinite nature without drawing living beings; reflects advanced mathematics predating Western discoveries
  • Arabic calligraphy — the highest art form, since it renders the literal Word of Allah beautifully (Naskh, Thuluth, Diwani, Kufi scripts)
  • Arabesques — intertwining vine/floral patterns symbolising creation's infinite growth under one Creator
  • Mosque architecture — dome, minaret, muqarnas (honeycomb vaulting), courtyard each carry spiritual meaning; the mosque is the heart of worship, learning, and community
Three Monuments, Three Continents
  • Masjid al-Haram (Makkah, Asia) — Islam's global spiritual centre
  • Hassan II Mosque (Casablanca, Africa) — modern adaptation of Islamic architecture
  • Alhambra Palace (Granada, Europe) — artistic heights under Muslim Spain
Why No Figurative Art
  • Avoids any risk of idol worship (shirk) returning through images
  • Supported by Sahih Muslim, Hadith 91: "Allah is beautiful and He loves beauty" — beauty is encouraged, but channeled through pattern and calligraphy, not living figures
Quick Recall
Geometry, Calligraphy, Arabesque, MosqueMakkah / Casablanca / AlhambraNo figures = avoid idolatryHadith 91

Arabic Language & Literary Heritage
Why Arabic matters, four sciences (Nahw, Sarf, Balagha, Adab), other languages elevated.
▼
Why Arabic Is Essential
  • Quran, Hadith, and Fiqh were all revealed/written in Arabic
  • Without Arabic, direct engagement with primary sources is impossible — translation always loses precision
  • The Prophet said: "Love Arabic because I am Arab, the Quran is in Arabic, and the people of Paradise will speak Arabic" (Al-Tabarani)
Four Sciences of Arabic
  • Nahw — grammar, sentence structure
  • Sarf — morphology, word formation
  • Balagha — rhetoric, eloquence
  • Adab — literature, poetry and prose
Other Languages Elevated
  • Persian — became the language of Islamic philosophy and poetry (e.g. Rumi)
  • Urdu — emerged as a synthesis of Arabic, Persian, and South Asian languages
  • Turkish & Swahili — also became vehicles of Islamic scholarship in their regions
  • Shows Islam's linguistic inclusivity — enriching languages rather than erasing them
Quick Recall

Halal & Haram Food System
Principles (Q2:168, Q5:3), four Haram categories, modern $3 trillion Halal industry.
▼
Quranic Principles
  • Quran 2:168 commands: "Eat of that which is lawful and good on the earth"
  • Quran 5:3 details what is forbidden
  • For food to be Halal: no pork, no intoxicants, slaughtered by a Muslim/Christian/Jew in Allah's name (Tasmiyah), and no blood eaten
Four Haram Categories
  • Pork — forbidden in Quran 2:173
  • Blood — forbidden under Quran 5:3
  • Carrion — animals not properly slaughtered
  • Intoxicating drinks — forbidden under Quran 5:90
Modern Relevance
  • Global Halal food industry today is valued at over $3 trillion
  • Shows 7th-century divine law still drives massive real economic activity
  • Demonstrates the enduring civilisational and economic relevance of Islamic dietary law worldwide

Five Pillars of Islamic Practice
Shahadah, Salah, Zakah, Sawm, Hajj — definition, basis, and social benefit each.
▼
Shahadah (Declaration of Faith)
  • Definition: "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His Messenger"
  • Basis: foundation of Tawhid (Quran 112:1–4)
  • Social role: the single entry point into Islam, giving every Muslim the same shared identity
Salah (Prayer)
  • Definition: five daily prayers at fixed times
  • Basis: Quran 29:45 — prayer restrains immorality and wrongdoing
  • Social role: congregational prayer (especially Jumu'ah) unites the community and reinforces equality
Zakah (Charity)
  • Definition: 2.5% of savings above the nisab threshold, paid yearly
  • Basis: second Pillar after Salah, central to Islamic economic justice
  • Social role: directly redistributes wealth from rich to poor — a built-in welfare system
Sawm (Fasting)
  • Definition: fasting dawn to sunset throughout Ramadan
  • Basis: Quran 2:183
  • Social role: builds collective empathy and increases charity and shared Iftar meals, strengthening social bonds
Hajj (Pilgrimage)
  • Definition: pilgrimage to Makkah once in a lifetime, for those able
  • Basis: Quran 3:97
  • Social role: unites Muslims of every race and nation in one place — universal brotherhood beyond personal spirituality
Quick Recall
Shahadah = identitySalah = connectionZakah = redistributionSawm = discipline + empathyHajj = global brotherhood

Buddhism's Non-Theism vs Islam's Path to Jannah
What non-theistic means, the Buddha, Four Noble Truths vs Q2:286 and Bukhari #6463.
▼
What "Non-Theistic" Means
  • No belief in a creator God who made the universe
  • No divine judge who weighs good and bad deeds
  • Liberation depends entirely on a person's own effort, not God's grace
Who the Buddha Was
  • Siddhartha Gautama, a human being (not divine)
  • Attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree, c. 528 BCE
  • Never claimed prophethood or divinity — only that he found a path to end suffering
Buddhist Path: Four Noble Truths → Eightfold Path
  • Life involves suffering (dukkha); it is caused by craving (tanha); suffering can end; the Eightfold Path ends it
  • Karma operates automatically — no God grants or denies Nirvana
Islamic Path: Faith + Deeds + Mercy
  • Jannah needs THREE things together: sincere faith (Iman), righteous deeds (Amal Salih), and Allah's mercy
  • Quran 2:286 — Allah does not burden a soul beyond what it can bear; it gets what it has earned
  • Sahih al-Bukhari #6463 — "None enters Paradise by deeds alone"; Allah's mercy is essential
Key Difference
  • Buddhism: self-achieved liberation, no divine role
  • Islam: liberation needs God's mercy in addition to faith and good deeds
Quick Recall
No creator GodBuddha = human, self-enlightenedFour Noble Truths → NirvanaIslam: Faith+Deeds+Mercy (Q2:286, Bukhari #6463)

Life After Death — Islam vs Hinduism vs Buddhism
Barzakh → Qiyamah → Mizan → Sirat → Jannah/Jahannam, then compared.
▼
Islamic Sequence (in order)
  • Barzakh — waiting state between death and resurrection
  • Yawm al-Qiyamah — Judgment Day, all souls resurrected (Quran 3:185)
  • Mizan — the Scale, every deed weighed, even an atom's weight (Quran 99:7–8)
  • Sirat — a bridge crossed over Hell
  • Final destination: Jannah or Jahannam, though some may be purified and later enter Paradise
Hinduism — Moksha & Reincarnation
  • Soul (Atman) reborn repeatedly (Samsara) based on accumulated karma
  • No single judgment — only cause and effect across many lives
  • Goal: Moksha — liberation, Atman merges back into Brahman
Buddhism — Nirvana & Rebirth
  • No permanent soul exists (Anatta)
  • Karma-energy flows into a new being after death — no creator God judges
  • Goal: Nirvana — complete ending of the rebirth cycle
Key Comparison
  • Islam: one life, one judgment, one God deciding the outcome
  • Hinduism & Buddhism: many lives, no single judgment, liberation through one's own karma/effort
Quick Recall
Barzakh → Qiyamah → Mizan → SiratHindu: Samsara → MokshaBuddhist: rebirth → Nirvana, no judge

Judaism — Closest to Islamic Monotheism
The Shema, Torah/Talmud, three ethics, Islamic view (Q5:44, Q2:87).
▼
The Jewish Concept of God (Shema)
  • "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One" — strict, indivisible monotheism
  • No Trinity or incarnation — theologically closest to Islamic Tawhid
Torah and Talmud
  • Torah — Five Books of Moses, the primary written revelation
  • Tanakh — Torah + Prophets + Writings
  • Talmud — Oral Law (Mishnah + Gemara commentary)
Three Core Ethics
  • Tikkun Olam — "repairing the world" through justice
  • Tzedakah — charity as a religious obligation, not optional
  • Prohibition of Lashon Hara — strict ban on gossip/evil speech
Islamic Position on Judaism
  • Honours Moses and Abraham as great prophets
  • Original Torah accepted as genuine revelation — Quran 5:44
  • But parts were altered by people over time — Quran 2:87
  • Both faiths share strong emphasis on law, charity, and the Oneness of God
Quick Recall
